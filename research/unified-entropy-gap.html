The Unified Entropy Gap of the Collatz Problem

One constant governs both possible failure modes of the conjecture — cycles and divergence — and it is a Kullback–Leibler divergence.

1. The constant

Let θ = log23 = 1.5849625007… and let H(x) = −x log2x − (1−x)log2(1−x) be the binary entropy. Define

κ  =  1 − H(1/θ)  =  0.050044472812… bits per halving
κ′  =  θκ  =  0.079318612775… bits per odd step

Closed forms (verified to 12 digits):

κ′ = θ − θ log2θ + (θ−1)log2(θ−1),     κ = 1 − log2θ + (1 − 1/θ)log2(θ−1).

And the conceptually cleanest form. Writing Geom(ρ) for the geometric law P(k) = ρ(1−ρ)k−1 on {1, 2, 3, …}:

κ′  =  DKL ( Geom(1/θ)  ∥  Geom(1/2) )

Geom(1/2), mean 2, is the law of the halving exponents e = v2(3n+1) that 2-adic dynamics actually provides (the parity-vector map is measure-preserving). Geom(1/θ), mean θ, is the most favorable law a counterexample is allowed to experience. The unified gap is the information-theoretic price, per odd step, of being a counterexample.

Literature note (added 2026-07): the large-deviation treatment of 3x+1 stopping times is classical — Lagarias & Weiss, The 3x+1 problem: two stochastic models, Ann. Appl. Probability 2 (1992) 229–261, whose rate-function analysis (extremal constants γ_BP ≈ 41.6776, ones-ratio 0.6091 for maximal trajectories) contains the same Cramér machinery. The contribution here reduces to the explicit unified formulation: one constant, stated as a KL divergence between geometric laws, governing both the cycle counting deficit (per halving) and the divergence density (per odd step) at the same slab boundary.

2. Where the same constant appears twice

Theorem A — cycles pay κ per halving

Integer cycles with A odd steps and B halvings correspond exactly to admissible patterns 0 = E0 < … < EA−1 ≤ B−1 with D | C, where D = 2B−3A and C = Σi 3A−i2Ei−1. Since B/A is pinned to θ (verification limit + forced signature), the number of patterns is &binom;(B−1, A−1) = 2H(1/θ)B + o(B), while D = 2B + o(B). The pattern count falls short of the modulus by exactly

2−κB + o(B),   κ = 1 − H(1/θ).

With B ≥ 14.2 billion (rigorous minimum signature), the naive expected number of cycles is ≤ 2−710,968,598.

Theorem B — divergence pays κ′ per odd step

A divergent orbit must keep bm = e1+…+em ≤ m(θ+δ) forever (δ ≈ 2−69), i.e., average halving exponent ≤ θ against a generic mean of 2. The first m exponents determine the start mod 2bm exactly, so the density of starting values surviving m odd steps is at most

Sm = Σb ≤ mθ &binom;(b−1, m−1) 2−b = 2−κ′m + o(m).

By Cramér / maximum entropy: the optimal survival strategy is exactly the tilted law Geom(1/θ), and the exponential cost is its KL divergence from Geom(1/2) — which is κ′. The divergent set has density zero at explicit rate.

Theorem C — unification

κ′ = θκ. Both failure modes sit on the same slab boundary b = θm: a cycle is a trajectory that closes on the boundary, a divergent orbit is one that stays below it. The entropy deficit is one and the same quantity, measured per halving (κ, cycles) or per odd step (κ′, divergence). Every route to a counterexample pays the identical information-theoretic toll.

Consequence — congruences are perfectly blind

Modulo powers of two, every odd residue is realized by genuine 2-adic cycles (No-Congruence Theorem); modulo fixed odd primes, C equidistributes exponentially fast with explicit spectral gaps. The arithmetic of the cycle equation carries no usable congruence information in either direction. What separates truth from counterexample lives only at the full modulus D ≈ 2B, in the sparse regime where patterns number 2(1−κ)B < D — exactly the regime no equidistribution technique currently reaches.

4. A first piece of individual-level structure: the Affine Renewal Identity

Theorem D — Affine Renewal Identity (exact, all moduli)

The shift σ: (E1,…,EA−1) → (E1+1,…,EA−1+1) is a bijection from {patterns with EA−1 ≤ B−2} onto {patterns with E1 ≥ 2}, and satisfies the exact integer identity C(σE) = 2C(E) − 3A−1. Hence, writing φ(r) = 2r − 3A−1 mod M, for every modulus M and every residue r:

N(φ(r)) − Nfirst(φ(r))  =  N(r) − Nlast(r)

where N counts patterns with C ≡ r, and Nfirst/Nlast restrict to E1 = 1 / EA−1 = B−1. Verified exhaustively at the real modulus M = D for four signatures, all residues (script 155). Telescoping around the full φ-orbit (length ordD(2)) yields the exact global constraint Σorbit (Nfirst − Nlast) = 0, and expresses N(0) — the number of cycles — through boundary counts at all recursion depths. Notably, 2B ≡ 3A (mod D) ties the orbit structure of φ directly to the cycle equation.

Three verified consequences (script 156)

1. Fixed-Point Corollary. φ fixes r* = 3A−1, forcing Nfirst(r*) = Nlast(r*) exactly — verified at four signatures, including non-trivial values (2 = 2, 1 = 1).

2. Rotation-Divisibility Dichotomy. Rotation maps solutions to solutions (Crot = (3C + D)/2e1, exactly), and a primitive cycle contributes exactly A rotated patterns. Hence N(0) = Σd | gcd(A,B) (A/d)·P(A/d, B/d). Since gcd(Amin, Bmin) = 1 at the minimal admissible signature: the solution count there is zero or at least 8,963,457,697 — all-or-nothing, against a global average of 2−7.1×108 per residue.

3. Renewal Smoothness. Along the φ-orbit of 0 (length ordD(2); the orbit itself equidistributes, KS = 0.005), consecutive N-values are strongly correlated: lag-1 autocorrelation +0.45 against a null threshold of 0.02 — the identity forces N to move only by boundary increments, making N “Lipschitz along the φ-flow.” Also verified: ord(3)/gcd(ord(3), A) divides ord(2), the trace of 2B ≡ 3A in the group structure.

Theorem E — Orbit-Sum Invariance (proved; scripts 157–158)

Let P(u) = Σk S(2ku) be the character-sum total over the doubling orbit of u mod D. Rotation is a bijection on patterns with Crot ≡ 3·2−e1C (mod D); summing over a full doubling orbit absorbs the 2−e1 twist by reindexing, leaving

P(3u) = P(u)   for all u.

Hence D·N(0) = Σt S(t) collapses to a short sum of class invariants on the double quotient (ℤ/D)×/⟨2,3⟩ at each divisor level — small because ord(2), ord(3) are large and 3A ≡ 2B ties the generators together. Verified exactly: at (10,16) the predicted 3 independent primitive values are observed; at (12,20) the predicted 4 classes merge under conjugation into the observed 24+24 orbit multiplicities. (The large divisor-level z-scores also found in script 158 are finite-B transients of size S·γpB, not asymptotic bias.) The cycle count is thereby a linear form in a handful of Gauss-period-type invariants per divisor level — the sharpest exact reformulation this program reached; bounding those invariants individually remains open.

Corollary — the prime-D reduction (script 159)

Across nine signatures: k3 (the order of 3’s image in (ℤ/D)×/⟨2⟩) divides A in every case — the cycle equation’s group-theoretic fingerprint — and the Ramanujan sum rule holds exactly everywhere. When D is prime and ⟨2,3⟩ generates all units (observed at (5,8) and (13,21)), the single primitive invariant satisfies P = D·N(0) − S(0) exactly — verified: P = −35 = −S(0) and P = −125970 = −S(0). At such signatures the cycle problem is the statement that one Gauss-period-type number equals −S(0). The invariant values themselves show no universal clustering (normalized spread −20…+27): the sum rules are the only exact structure; pointwise bounds on the periods would be needed to go further, and those belong to open analytic number theory.

Why this matters — and its honest limit

These are exact, individual-level facts at the true modulus D — the first such structure in this program. But the “rigidity cascade” they suggest is self-consistent rather than self-contradictory: a hypothetical cycle forces its boundary fibers to concentrate as well (at least 41.5% of a cycle’s rotations have e1 = 1, since Σ(ei−1) = B−A ≈ 0.585A), so the recursion re-encodes the cycle’s tail at every depth instead of forbidding it. The identity constrains how a counterexample would sit in residue space — extreme, isolated, all-or-nothing — without yet excluding it. It is a lever, not a solution.

5. What this does and does not prove

Proved: the divergent set has density zero at rate 2−κ′m; any cycle has ≥ 8.96×109 odd elements with its signature forced to the line B = ⌈Aθ⌉; both failure modes face the identical entropy deficit; and no congruence — to any modulus, in any direction — can decide the problem.

Not proved: that the deficit is actually paid. The gap statements are density and counting statements; a single exceptional integer — one pattern with D | C, or one orbit riding the slab forever — is invisible to them. Converting “expected number 2−7×108” into “number zero” requires equidistribution of C mod D at modulus ≈ 2B, or an individual-orbit rigidity argument. Neither exists. That precise statement is, in our assessment, the sharpest available formulation of why the Collatz conjecture is hard.
